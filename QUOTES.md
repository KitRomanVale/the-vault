# Quote Notes

This file documents the attributed sayings used in The Vault's celebration messages. The uncredited celebration lines are original project text and are covered by the [MIT License](LICENSE).

Attributions are intentionally cautious: familiar internet credits are not always the original source, and wording can change between translations. Last reviewed 14 July 2026.

| In-app wording | In-app credit | Source note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| “A journey of a thousand miles begins with a single step.” | Laozi | A common English rendering of `千里之行，始於足下` (*Dao De Jing*, chapter 64); translations vary between “a thousand miles” and “a thousand li.” [Source text and translation](https://ctext.org/dao-de-jing/zh) |
| “Whatever you can do, or dream you can, begin it.” | W. H. Murray, after Goethe | Anster's very free 1835 translation of Goethe begins “What you can do”; Murray popularized the “Whatever” wording in *The Scottish Himalayan Expedition* (1951). [Attribution research](https://dergipark.org.tr/en/pub/iuaded/article/1350278) |
| “Well begun is half done.” | Greek proverb | Benjamin Jowett's translation of *Politics* 5.4 has Aristotle introduce this explicitly as a proverb. [Text](https://classics.mit.edu/Aristotle/politics.5.five.html) |
| “Start where you are. Use what you have. Do what you can.” | Arthur Ashe | Widely attributed to Ashe and reproduced by the Arthur Ashe Learning Center; the original occasion has not been identified here. [Arthur Ashe Learning Center](https://arthurashe.ucla.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/87/2018/06/aalc_activitybook_ages13to17.pdf) |
| “The palest ink is better than the best memory.” | Traditional saying | Commonly described as a Chinese proverb, but no dependable primary source or original Chinese wording was identified. Keep the credit generic rather than naming an author. |
| “The reward of a thing well done is to have done it.” | Emerson, after Seneca | The underlying Latin maxim is in Seneca's *Moral Letters* 81.20. Emerson later used this English form in “New England Reformers” (1844). [Seneca](https://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Aabo%3Aphi%2C1017%2C015%3A81) · [Emerson](https://www.walden.org/50-quotations-by-ralph-waldo-emerson/) |
| “Great things are done by a series of small things brought together.” | Vincent van Gogh | A condensed rendering of letter 274 to Theo van Gogh, dated 22 October 1882. [Van Gogh Letters](https://vangoghletters.org/vg/letters/let274/letter.html) |
| “It does not matter how slowly you go as long as you do not stop.” | After Confucius | A modern paraphrase, not a literal quotation, of *Analects* 9.20, where the related passage says that Yan Yuan constantly advanced and never stopped. [Source text and translation](https://ctext.org/analects/zi-han) |
| “Dripping water hollows out stone, not through force but through persistence.” | After Ovid | “A drop hollows the stone” appears in *Ex Ponto* 4.10.5; the explanation about force and persistence is a later expansion of the proverb. [Ovid's text](https://scaife.perseus.org/reader/urn%3Acts%3AlatinLit%3Aphi0959.phi009.perseus-lat2%3A4.10/) |
| “Little by little, one travels far.” | Spanish proverb | An English rendering of variants such as *Poco a poco, se va lejos*. [Instituto Cervantes proverb record](https://cvc.cervantes.es/lengua/refranero/ficha.aspx?Lng=9&Par=59285) |

When a quote or its displayed credit changes in `the-vault.html`, update this table at the same time.
